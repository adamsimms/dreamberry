Dreamberry

About

A machine present at a place it can no longer photograph — and the question of whether that presence is a gift or a consolation prize.

What it is

Dreamberry is a live generative window onto a view of Pinchard's Island, Newfoundland, from a small family cabin. It is the successor to Cloudberry, an off-the-grid, solar-powered camera that photographed the same frame once an hour from 2017 until it failed in the winter of 2018. That archive is closed and sacred. Dreamberry does not add to it — it dreams from it.

Every hour, Dreamberry reads the current weather at the island and generates a single image of the remembered window under that weather, anchored to the real Cloudberry frames so the rocks, horizon, and cabin-edge stay recognizable. The result is real atmosphere haunting a remembered frame — the last genuine live signal animating a fiction.

Every frame is generated

This is not a camera and not a photograph. There is no live feed from the island. Each image is synthesized by a model (SDXL with ControlNet and a real-frame anchor) and is labeled generated in the details drawer, always. The bar is honesty, not indistinguishable-from-a-photograph: a viewer who knows Cloudberry should recognize the same window under plausible current weather, and know that a machine imagined it.

The weather is the only thing still true

The single genuinely-live signal is the atmosphere at the island right now — cloud, wind, fog, visibility, sea state, the angle of the sun. Everything visual is memory or invention; only the weather is real and current. That asymmetry is the honest core of the piece. The condition packet used for each frame is shown in the drawer.

When it cannot dream

Dreamberry inherits Cloudberry's honesty about failure rather than hiding it:

  • Weather silence. If the weather feeds go quiet or stale, Dreamberry does not invent a frame. It holds the last dream and waits — presence stuck in time, like the camera that never woke.
  • Signal lost. If the generator itself fails, the window shows static — a dead channel, honestly, until the next hour returns.
  • Identity collapse. When the model cannot hold the window, the image dissolves rather than inventing a false, sharp place. At its most dreamed, that dissolve is the truest state, not an error.

The ground it stands on

Pinchard's Island sits on the island of Newfoundland — Ktaqmkuk — the unceded, ancestral territory of the Beothuk and the Mi'kmaq. It was resettled by the artist's family in the mid-twentieth century, and Dreamberry is a settler apparatus trained to synthetically re-occupy that colonized ground. A machine hallucinating presence there is not neutral; it carries a debt that this work names and does not pretend to resolve.

Who

Dreamberry is by Adam Simms, extending a long collaboration with his family in documenting the return to Pinchard's Island. The dream dial — how hard the machine is allowed to dream the place — is held by the artist for now; the door stays open for the family whose story this is to hold it in future.
